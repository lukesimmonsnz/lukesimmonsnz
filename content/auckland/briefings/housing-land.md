## 2. The physical substrate — what doesn't change

Auckland sits on an **isthmus between two harbours** (Waitematā to the east, Manukau to the west), roughly 2 km wide at its narrowest point. That geometry is permanent on any civilisational timescale and is the single most consequential fact about the city's land.

**Enduring physical constraints:**
- **Two harbours** frame the city north–south. Crossings (Harbour Bridge, Mangere Bridge, future crossings) are chokepoints that shape where land is valuable.
- **The Auckland Volcanic Field** — roughly 53 scoria cones, maars and tuff rings across the isthmus. The field is geologically **active**, not extinct — GNS Science treats future eruptions as a real, if low-annual-probability, possibility. `[verify: current GNS eruption probability estimate]` Many cones are protected reserves, viewshafts, or regional parks.
- **Waitākere Ranges** (west, ~16,000 ha) and **Hunua Ranges** (south-east, ~17,000 ha) are regional parks — essentially permanent removals from the developable land base. `[verify: exact hectarages]`
- **Coastlines** — roughly 3,700 km of coastline in the Auckland region, much of it at risk from sea level rise on 50–100 year horizons.
- **Topography** — ridgelines, steep valleys, extensive unstable soils in the west, alluvial flood-prone areas across the south and north-west. Less flat developable land than the region's size suggests.

**What's changing on a 100-year horizon:**
- **Sea level rise** — NIWA's projections imply 0.3–1.2 m by 2100 depending on emissions pathway. That erases or makes uninsurable meaningful stretches of low-lying coastal land — parts of the Tāmaki estuary, Mangere, Hobsonville, Weymouth, north-shore bays. `[verify: NIWA coastal hazard maps for Auckland]`
- **Flood-prone zones** — the January 2023 Auckland Anniversary floods made the existing flood-plain maps politically visible. Insurance retreat is already starting and will accelerate.
- **Heat, drought, fire** — urban heat islands worsen with density and climate warming; Waitākere fire risk is rising.

**Implication for a 10/50/100-year frame:** Auckland's *physically developable* land base is smaller than its administrative area suggests, and the effective developable area is shrinking at the coastal margin even as population grows.

## 3. Mana whenua and pre-colonial land

Tāmaki Makaurau — "Tāmaki of a thousand lovers" — was one of the most contested and most populous regions of pre-colonial Aotearoa, valued for its twin-harbour portage, volcanic soils, fishing grounds, and strategic position.

**Iwi with mana whenua interests in the Auckland isthmus and surrounds include:**
- **Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei** and wider Ngāti Whātua (central isthmus)
- **Te Kawerau ā Maki** (Waitākere and west)
- **Ngāi Tai ki Tāmaki** (east and Hauraki Gulf)
- **Ngāti Pāoa** and the wider **Marutūāhu confederation** (east, Hauraki, inner Gulf)
- **Waikato–Tainui** (south and south-west, overlapping with Ngāti Te Ata, Ngāti Tamaoho)
- **Te Ākitai Waiohua** (Māngere, Ihumātao, parts of south)
- Multiple others with specific site-based interests

Overlapping interests are the norm, not the exception, and are a permanent feature of Auckland's land politics. Any framing that implies a single "mana whenua" voice is wrong.

**Pre-colonial land concepts** differed fundamentally from European freehold: rights were relational, seasonal, layered (fishing, gardening, burial, pā), and embedded in whakapapa. Translating those concepts into individualised title — which the Crown did from 1865 — was itself an act of land alienation, not merely a record of it.

## 4. Colonisation, alienation, confiscation

A compressed factual summary — the public page will need fuller treatment:

- **1840 Treaty of Waitangi** — signed in both te reo Māori and English, with crucial translation differences around *kāwanatanga* / *tino rangatiratanga* still contested in their implications.
- **1840s Crown purchases** — Ngāti Whātua offered land at the Waitematā for the capital; subsequent Crown purchases across the isthmus were often contested in scale, boundaries, and consent.
- **1863 New Zealand Settlements Act** — confiscation (*raupatu*) of Waikato-Tainui land south of the Auckland isthmus after the invasion of the Waikato. Portions of what is now south Auckland fall under this history.
- **Native Land Court (from 1865)** — individualised customary title, enabling piecemeal sale. This is the single largest mechanism of Māori land loss in the Auckland region, larger than direct confiscation.
- **20th-century urban displacement** — public works takings, motorway construction, urban renewal schemes (e.g. Ōrākei / Takaparawhau 1951 eviction; Freemans Bay and Ponsonby "slum clearance"; motorway severance of inner suburbs).

By the late 20th century, the overwhelming majority of Auckland region land was in Crown or private pākehā hands.

## 5. Treaty settlements and contemporary iwi land

Treaty settlements since the 1990s have returned meaningful (though modest relative to historical loss) land, commercial assets, and cultural redress. Key Auckland-area settlements include:

- **Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei Claims Settlement Act 2012** — includes commercial redress and right-of-first-refusal over Crown land.
- **Tāmaki Makaurau Collective Redress Act 2014** — collective settlement covering 13 iwi/hapū with interests in maunga (volcanic cones), giving co-governance of the 14 tūpuna maunga via the Tūpuna Maunga Authority.
- **Ngāi Tai ki Tāmaki**, **Te Kawerau ā Maki**, **Ngāti Pāoa**, **Marutūāhu**, **Waiohua–Tāmaki** settlements — each with differing scale and instruments.
- **Waikato–Tainui** (1995, 2010) — applies to south Auckland areas.
- **Hauraki collective settlements** — ongoing implementation.

Post-settlement iwi are now **significant Auckland landowners and developers**:
- Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei has built and operates 700+ homes in Ōrākei, with further development planned. `[verify: current figure]`
- Multiple iwi hold commercial property portfolios and are increasingly active in master-planned housing (e.g. Hobsonville-area developments, south Auckland iwi-led papakāinga).

**This is a durable structural shift.** Over 50–100 years, iwi are likely to be among the most consequential long-term landowners and developers in the region, because unlike most private landowners they are **intergenerationally constrained** — they cannot simply sell and exit.

## 6. Contemporary ownership pattern

Rough cut of who owns Auckland land today (shares are illustrative; a proper version will need LINZ data):

- **Private freehold residential** — the large majority of urban land by area; highly fragmented ownership (≈500,000+ titles).
- **Private freehold rural** — lifestyle blocks, farms, forestry on the fringes.
- **Auckland Council & CCOs** — regional parks (Waitākere, Hunua, many smaller), local parks, community facilities, stormwater reserves, Watercare infrastructure land, Ports of Auckland land, AT corridors.
- **Crown** — Department of Conservation estate (small in Auckland region vs. e.g. the West Coast), Kāinga Ora housing portfolio, state highways (NZTA), Defence land, schools.
- **Iwi / post-settlement entities** — growing; concentrated in specific areas including Ōrākei, Ihumātao vicinity, south Auckland, Hauraki Gulf islands.
- **Māori freehold land (Te Ture Whenua)** — small share of Auckland region, typically multiply-owned, development-constrained by title complexity.
- **Heritage trusts, churches, universities, hospitals** — non-trivial and often protected from sale.
- **Landbanked private holdings** — zoned-but-undeveloped residential land held for capital gain rather than use. Scale is debated; mattering more in the Future Urban Zone.

Two structural features stand out:
1. **Fragmentation** — tens of thousands of small private titles make assembly of development-sized parcels expensive, slow, and sometimes impossible.
2. **Title-locked land** — significant parcels cannot easily change use: Treaty-settlement lands with cultural covenants, heritage-listed sites, regional parks, Tūpuna Maunga, DOC reserves. These **should** be understood as permanent fixtures of the developable map.

## 7. The regulatory layer over the top

Zoning and the RMA / its successor sit on top of physical land and decide what can be built where.

- **Pre-2016:** patchwork of eight legacy district plans from the pre-amalgamation councils, wildly inconsistent.
- **2016 Auckland Unitary Plan (AUP):** single integrated plan; major upzoning; introduced the Mixed Housing Suburban, Mixed Housing Urban, and Terrace Housing & Apartment Buildings zones. The AUP is the most consequential land-use decision in the region in a generation.
- **Special character overlays:** heritage/character restrictions on demolition and form, concentrated in the inner isthmus (Ponsonby, Grey Lynn, Mt Eden, Devonport, Parnell, etc.). These protect existing built form and constrain intensification.
- **Viewshafts:** protected sightlines to volcanic cones, constraining height in specific corridors.
- **Rural Urban Boundary (RUB) / metropolitan urban limits:** the line between where urban development is expected and where it is not.
- **Future Urban Zone (FUZ):** land designated for eventual urbanisation, awaiting infrastructure and structure-planning.
- **Resource Management Act reform:** the RMA is being replaced — what succeeds it will shape the next generation of land-use decisions. On a 100-year view, the *fact of* replacement matters more than which statute wins the current round.

**The durable point for a 100-year frame:** regulatory regimes come and go; the **physical inventory** of land categories (character, maunga, harbour-adjacent, flood-prone, Māori-title-constrained, iwi-settlement) persists far longer than any particular statute.

## 8. Structural features that persist across political cycles

Distilling the above into the features a reader in 2076 or 2126 will still recognise:

1. **Isthmus geometry and twin harbours** — permanent; everything routes around these.
2. **Volcanic field and viewshafts** — cones are culturally and legally protected; viewshafts will persist.
3. **Ranges as land banks of last resort** — Waitākere and Hunua are politically near-impossible to develop. Conservation estate is sticky.
4. **Climate-driven loss of coastal developable land** — accelerating through the century.
5. **Heritage character zones in the inner isthmus** — once designated, very sticky. A 100-year plan should assume these remain.
6. **Fragmented private freehold** — high assembly costs will continue to shape what gets built.
7. **Iwi as permanent, intergenerational landowners** — growing share, long time-horizon, non-exiting.
8. **Auckland Council as the largest single strategic landowner** — via parks, reserves, infrastructure corridors, and CCOs. Whoever runs it, the portfolio is the portfolio.
9. **Central Crown holdings** (KO, NZTA, Defence, schools) — subject to national political cycles but slow to change in aggregate.
10. **Regulatory regime instability** — one near-certainty is that the RMA successor will itself be replaced within 50 years. Build the analysis to be regime-agnostic.

## 9. The durable long-run questions

Questions that still matter in 2076 regardless of who is in government:

- **How much of Auckland's land base should be permanently held in conservation / open space / ecological function?** (Trade-off with housing supply, carbon, biodiversity, amenity, climate resilience.)
- **What is the right long-run split between intensification of the existing urban footprint and greenfield expansion at the metropolitan edge or in satellite towns?**
- **How do we enable development of multiply-owned Māori land without alienating it?** (Title reform, lease-based models, iwi-led consolidation.)
- **Who captures the uplift when land changes use?** (Landowner, developer, community, Crown via LVT or betterment levy.)
- **How is climate-driven managed retreat funded, governed, and allocated?** Who pays? Who stays?
- **Should urban-limit regulation be tighter (protect ecology, force intensification) or looser (cheaper housing, more sprawl)?**
- **How much of the developable land should be in public, iwi, and community hands vs. private freehold?** (A 100-year ownership-mix question.)
- **How do we value the permanence of place — cones, heritage, mana whenua sites — against the urgency of the housing need?**

These are the spine of the public Land page: framed as durable questions, with evidence and camps, not resolved.

## 10. Solution camps on land (neutral presentation)

Camps to be represented even-handedly on the public page:

### A. Intensification-first / compact city
**Core claim:** Auckland should grow within its existing urban footprint; peripheral land is ecologically and economically costly to urbanise.
**Flagship moves:** keep or tighten RUB, abolish remaining character overlays, upzone aggressively along rapid transit.
**Tensions with:** heritage preservation, rural landowner interests, household preference for detached housing.

### B. Greenfield / expansion
**Core claim:** intensification alone cannot deliver enough housing at acceptable cost; the metropolitan boundary must move.
**Flagship moves:** release FUZ faster, extend the RUB, invest in infrastructure to greenfield areas, build satellite towns.
**Tensions with:** ecology, carbon, infrastructure cost, productive rural land, climate-exposed peripheries.

### C. Satellite / polycentric
**Core claim:** neither central intensification nor edge sprawl alone; concentrate growth in defined nodes (Warkworth, Kumeu, Pukekohe, Drury).
**Flagship moves:** structure plans and rapid-transit investment to defined growth centres.
**Tensions with:** infrastructure cost of multiple centres, risk of low-density sprawl between nodes.

### D. Māori-led land use / papakāinga
**Core claim:** significant housing capacity exists on iwi-owned and Māori freehold land; the barriers are regulatory and financial, not physical.
**Flagship moves:** reform Te Ture Whenua Māori Act, fund papakāinga and iwi-led master plans, Crown as co-investor not deliverer.
**Tensions with:** non-Māori landowners' expectations, pace of Treaty-settlement implementation.

### E. Conservation-first / biophysical limits
**Core claim:** a growing share of Auckland's land should be permanently returned to ecological function; urban density should absorb growth.
**Flagship moves:** expand regional parks and reserves, permanent RUB, private-land conservation incentives (QEII-style covenants), biodiversity net-gain rules.
**Tensions with:** housing supply, affordability in the short term.

### F. Land-value taxation / land-market reform
**Core claim:** the structure of land rights and taxation, not zoning alone, determines land use over the long run.
**Flagship moves:** land value tax, betterment levy on rezoning uplift, vacant-land tax, reform of heritage compensation.
**Tensions with:** existing owners, valuation practicalities, political feasibility.

### G. Devolved-governance / institutional reform
**Core claim:** the binding constraint is the Crown–Council fiscal relationship and Auckland's limited ability to finance its own land-and-infrastructure strategy.
**Flagship moves:** Crown-backed wholesale borrowing for Council infrastructure, land-value-capture tools, greater constitutional autonomy for Auckland.
**Tensions with:** national-government political economy, inter-regional equity.

These camps overlap and combine in practice. A policy/academic reader will expect all seven represented without favour.
