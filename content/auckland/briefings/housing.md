## 2. Scale of the problem

Auckland is the centre of New Zealand's housing crisis — most of the national discourse, most of the political heat, and most of the unmet demand sit here.

**Prices**
- Auckland median sale price peaked around **NZ$1.30M in late 2021** (REINZ HPI basis), fell roughly **15–22%** through 2023, and has been in a shallow recovery since. `[verify: REINZ or CoreLogic current series]`
- Price-to-income ratios: Auckland has been in the 8–10× range for median household income for most of the last decade — among the least affordable cities in the OECD on Demographia's methodology. `[verify: Demographia 2025/26 edition]`

**Rents**
- Weekly rents rose ~25% between 2020 and 2023 before stabilising in 2024–25 as migration flattened. `[verify: MBIE tenancy bond data]`
- Rent-to-income ratios for lower-quintile households are the binding affordability problem — more so than house prices, which affect would-be buyers.

**Supply**
- Auckland consents have averaged **~14,000–18,000 dwellings/year** since 2021, down from a peak around 21,000 in 2022. `[verify: Stats NZ building consents]`
- Completions lag consents by 12–24 months and are materially lower.
- Academic work (Ryan Greenaway-McGrevy, University of Auckland) has argued the **2016 Unitary Plan upzoning produced a measurable supply response** — Auckland built more than it otherwise would have, and rents grew less than in comparable cities without upzoning. This is contested in its magnitude but widely cited.

**Homelessness and severe deprivation**
- 2023 Census counted roughly **5,000 people in severe housing deprivation** in the Auckland region (living without shelter, in temporary accommodation, or sharing severely). `[verify: 2023 Census housing deprivation release]`
- Transitional and emergency housing waitlists grew sharply 2020–23, then were pushed down by the 2024 government's tightening of eligibility — the *register* shrank, but the underlying need did not necessarily fall.

**Quality**
- A large share of Auckland's stock is pre-1980, poorly insulated, and damp. The Healthy Homes standards are lifting the rental floor but enforcement is patchy. Quality is an under-discussed dimension of the crisis.

## 3. Drivers — what's consensus, what's contested

### Broadly consensus
- **Land-use regulation** historically constrained supply (minimum lot sizes, height limits, character overlays, parking minimums). The Unitary Plan partially fixed this; remaining constraints (heritage overlays, viewshafts, infrastructure capacity) still bind.
- **Infrastructure** — water, wastewater, stormwater, transport — is a hard constraint on where new housing can actually be built. Watercare capacity limits have delayed or killed projects.
- **Construction costs** rose ~30–40% post-COVID and have only partially unwound. `[verify: Stats NZ CGPI construction]`
- **Demand surges** from migration and low interest rates (2020–21) were real and large.

### Genuinely contested
- **How much of the price run-up was supply vs. demand vs. credit.** Economists split. The RBNZ and Treasury lean toward credit + tax treatment as large; academic urbanists lean supply. Both are partly right; the weights matter for policy.
- **Whether upzoning actually lowers prices or just shifts where growth happens.** Greenaway-McGrevy argues yes for Auckland; some planners and heritage advocates dispute the causal attribution.
- **Role of foreign buyers.** Largely removed as a variable after the 2018 ban, but still cited in political rhetoric. Empirically minor post-2018.
- **Role of investors vs. owner-occupiers.** Tax settings (bright-line, interest deductibility) have swung repeatedly — the evidence on what actually moved is thinner than the political confidence suggests.

### My read
The supply story is the most load-bearing and the most actionable. Demand-side levers (migration, credit, tax) matter but are blunter, more politically fraught, and often operate at the national rather than Auckland level. The public page should lead with supply and infrastructure, treat credit/tax as national-level context, and be honest that no single lever explains the last twenty years.

## 4. Current responses

### Central government (as of late 2025)
- **Housing Supply Act 2021 / MDRS** — National-Labour bipartisan deal requiring tier-1 councils to permit 3 dwellings / 3 storeys on most residential sites. National in 2023–24 made MDRS **optional** for councils; Auckland Council has moved to partially opt out in parts of the isthmus. Live political fight.
- **Going for Housing Growth** — the Coalition's planning package: strengthen intensification near rapid transit, relax infrastructure funding tools, push councils harder on housing capacity.
- **Kāinga Ora** — Bill English-led review (2024) concluded KO was over-leveraged and under-performing on build cost. Build programme slowed, some asset sales, debate about privatisation vs. recapitalisation ongoing.
- **First Home Grant** — scrapped 2024. **Bright-line test** shortened back to 2 years. **Interest deductibility** for investors reinstated. Net effect: tilted back toward investors, away from first-home buyers, with the argument that landlord supply matters more than the marginal FHB subsidy.
- **Affordable Housing Fund / Build-to-Rent** — incremental, not transformative.

### Auckland Council
- **Unitary Plan** (2016) — still the single most consequential housing policy of the last decade.
- **Future Development Strategy** / **Auckland Plan 2050** — strategic documents; implementation is the question.
- **Watercare** capacity and DRCs (Development Response Contributions) — technical but decisive for whether projects pencil.
- **Partial MDRS opt-out** — signals a retreat from blanket intensification toward transit-corridor-focused intensification. Defensible or regressive depending on who you ask.

### Iwi and Māori housing providers
- **Papakāinga** developments on Māori land, supported by Te Puni Kōkiri funding and MAIHI (Māori and Iwi Housing Innovation) framework.
- **Te Matapihi** as a national Māori housing advocacy body.
- Generally under-resourced relative to need; often held back by RMA / title complexity on multiply-owned Māori land.

## 5. The main solution camps

I'd group the serious proposals into five camps. A policy/academic reader will expect all five to be represented fairly.

### A. Supply-side / YIMBY
**Core claim:** Auckland is still under-built because regulation, infrastructure funding, and risk pricing block housing where people want to live.
**Flagship proposals:** blanket upzoning near rapid transit, abolish remaining character overlays, infrastructure financing via targeted rates / value capture, faster consenting.
**Key advocates:** Greenaway-McGrevy, Greater Auckland, The Helen Clark Foundation (selectively), parts of Treasury.
**Weakness:** political (heritage/character is a real preference for many voters); assumes construction sector can scale; does little for the bottom of the market in the short run.

### B. Public housing expansion
**Core claim:** the market will never deliver affordable housing at the lowest income deciles; only a large state builder can.
**Flagship proposals:** recapitalise Kāinga Ora, target 30–40k new state/community houses over a decade, community housing provider (CHP) scale-up.
**Key advocates:** Greens, parts of Labour, Community Housing Aotearoa.
**Weakness:** fiscal; KO's cost-per-unit is high; crowding-out debates.

### C. Tax / land reform
**Core claim:** distorted tax treatment of housing (no CGT, favourable treatment of rental investment) drives speculation and misallocates capital.
**Flagship proposals:** capital gains tax, land value tax, removing remaining investor tax advantages, rate reform.
**Key advocates:** TOP, Max Rashbrooke, parts of academia, NZ Initiative (on LVT specifically — interesting right-left overlap).
**Weakness:** transition problems; political third rail for CGT; LVT technically harder than proponents admit in a mass-revaluation NZ context.

### D. Demand-side / population
**Core claim:** migration-driven demand has consistently outrun supply; manage that and the problem eases.
**Flagship proposals:** lower net migration settings, stricter investor visa conditions, tighter foreign buyer rules.
**Key advocates:** NZ First, parts of the Greens (on environmental grounds), some economists.
**Weakness:** growth/productivity tradeoffs; treats symptom not cause; discriminatory optics.

### E. Māori-led / tino rangatiratanga
**Core claim:** solutions for Māori housing need Māori-controlled delivery on Māori land, with Crown as funder not deliverer.
**Flagship proposals:** expand papakāinga funding, RMA reform for multiply-owned Māori land, iwi-based CHPs.
**Key advocates:** Te Matapihi, iwi housing arms, Māori Party.
**Weakness:** scale — addresses a specific (important) segment, not the whole Auckland market.

A serious site will treat these as **complements, not alternatives**. The honest answer is some of each. The interesting editorial question is which gets the loudest voice on the public page.

## 6. Where the actual debate is, right now

If you dropped into a housing policy room in Auckland in April 2026, the live fights are:

1. **Is Auckland Council's MDRS opt-out defensible?** Transit-focused intensification vs. blanket upzoning.
2. **What should Kāinga Ora's target build rate be** post-English review, and should assets be sold to fund it or is that eating the seed corn?
3. **Infrastructure funding** — are targeted rates / value-capture tools sufficient, or does the Crown need to directly fund Watercare-scale investment?
4. **RMA replacement** — the coalition's planning reforms (Natural and Built Environments Act replacement) will reshape everything; detail still contested.
5. **Is the market now supply-constrained or demand-constrained?** Consents are down, migration is down; the answer decides whether you push harder on zoning or wait for the cycle.

A defensible first-draft thesis for Luke to accept, reject, or sharpen:

> Auckland's housing crisis is primarily a supply problem caused by decades of restrictive zoning, underfunded infrastructure, and procyclical credit, layered on top of genuine demand pressures. The 2016 Unitary Plan worked; the current retreat from blanket intensification risks undoing it. The public housing sector is too small to be a market-maker; recapitalising Kāinga Ora and scaling community housing providers is necessary for the bottom of the market. Tax reform — particularly land value taxation — would do more than any other single policy to realign incentives, but is politically blocked. Māori-led housing on Māori land is under-delivered relative to need and should be prioritised in infrastructure and planning reform.
