## Why technology on a 100-year frame

Most discussion of "AI for cities" operates on a one-to-five-year horizon — pilots, optimisation, productivity dashboards. A century is longer than the useful life of any current architecture, framework, model family, or company. Framing the question in terms of *durable levers* — what computer science and engineering can do that survives the churn — filters out the hype and surfaces work worth starting now.

This page is an outline awaiting expansion. The problems named here map to the structural issues raised on the [housing pages](../../housing/) and on the [last 100 years](../last-100-years/) retrospective; the point is to pair each with a technical lever that is durable rather than fashionable.

## Housing: automated design, modular construction, digital permitting

The fundamental bottleneck in Auckland housing is not land or capital but the slow, artisanal process of design, consenting, and building. Levers worth tracking on a long horizon: generative / rule-based design that produces compliant plans in minutes; factory-built modular components with standard interfaces; machine-checked building consents that flip the default from "weeks of human review" to "automated pass unless flagged". None of these are new in isolation; the 100-year question is whether they compound into a different construction industry.

## Transport: networks as the substrate for autonomy

Autonomous vehicles are often treated as the dominant question, but the slower and more durable work is in the *network layer*: lane discipline, signal coordination, kerb management, real-time ridesharing dispatch, freight routing. Each of those is a CS problem with a long build time and long payoff. The 100-year question is not "will the cars drive themselves" but "can Auckland's road, rail, ferry and cycle network be operated as a single optimisation problem."

## Infrastructure: digital twins, predictive maintenance, sensor networks

Water, stormwater, power and roading networks decay in ways that are largely invisible until failure. Dense sensor instrumentation, combined with physics-based models and ML-driven anomaly detection, turns the maintenance problem from reactive to predictive. The long-horizon lever is the persistent *digital twin* of the infrastructure stock — built once, maintained as a first-class asset, and kept current across the lifetime of the physical system.

## Environment: climate modelling, coastal adaptation, energy

Auckland's coastal exposure, weather-driven flood risk, and energy transition are all problems with explicit physical models and enormous volumes of relevant data. Machine-learning surrogates for expensive climate simulators, satellite-derived flood-plain tracking, optimisation of a distributed renewable grid — each is a concrete CS contribution to a problem the city will still face in 2125.

## Governance and participation

The less visible but possibly most important lever: tools that let large groups of residents produce decisions that hold up to adversarial scrutiny. This includes open structured data pipelines (of which this Auckland project is a small example), participatory budgeting tooling, and machine-legible policy. The 100-year question is whether a city of three or four million people can be governed through something more accountable than the current mix of public consultation forms and elected-member discretion.

## What *not* to rely on

A durable 100-year list also requires honesty about what is likely to be ephemeral. Specific model families, specific vendors, specific programming languages — none of these will survive the horizon. The work that compounds is in *interfaces*, *standards*, *data formats*, and *institutional capacity to maintain systems*. This page is an argument for betting long on the unglamorous layer, not on whichever chatbot is fashionable in any given year.

## How this site fits in

The Auckland research project itself is a small experiment in one of these levers — content-as-data with schema validation and lint-checkable citations — applied to public-policy argument. It is one data point for what the durable governance layer might look like when built in the open. If that experiment holds up, the patterns used here should be transferable to other cities and other domains, which is part of the point.
