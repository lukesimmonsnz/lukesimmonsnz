# Affordability


## Introduction

Land is the stock. Supply Economics is the flow. Affordability is
the **outcome** — what Aucklanders actually pay for housing, how
that compares with what they earn, and who bears the cost when the
ratio is poor. This page sits alongside its siblings in the Housing
section and assumes the reader has either read them or will. The
supply-side fixes live in Land and Supply Economics, the public
landlord policy in Public Housing, the mana-whenua-specific
pathways in Māori Housing, and the tax instruments in Tax Reform.
What is left to this page is measurement, distributional incidence,
and the interventions that act on the outcome rather than on stock
or flow.

Affordability is fundamentally a **ratio problem**. It is the
relationship between what housing costs and what households earn —
never either in isolation. A city can become more affordable
through cheaper housing, higher earnings, or both. The arithmetic
does not permit a third option.


## Measuring affordability

There is no single number that captures affordability. There is a
family of related ratios, each with different policy implications,
and a document like this has to name which measure it uses because
conclusions differ between them.

This page uses three measures in combination:

- **Price-to-income ratio (PTI)** — median dwelling price divided
  by median household income. Useful for describing the entry
  barrier to ownership. The Demographia methodology uses five times
  as the "severely unaffordable" threshold; Auckland has sat at
  eight to ten times for most of the last decade.
- **Rent-to-income ratio** — median weekly rent divided by median
  weekly household income, calibrated against standard cost-burden
  thresholds (thirty per cent for burden, fifty per cent for
  severe burden). The decision-relevant metric for the roughly
  one in three Auckland households who rent.
- **Homeownership rate by cohort** — the share of people in a
  given age band who own their home, tracked over decades. The
  clearest signal of structural affordability drift over a
  generation or more.

Other measures — residual income after housing, deposit gap,
severe housing deprivation — appear where they illuminate
something the three primary metrics miss. Severe deprivation is
the extreme tail and is covered in depth on the Homelessness
subpage.

The canonical long-run Auckland PTI series is the annual
[Demographia International Housing Affordability
Survey](http://www.demographia.com/db-dhi-index.pdf), published each January
as a PDF. The same ratio can also be reconstructed from Stats NZ median
household income and RBNZ / Stats NZ house-price indices. No long-run chart
is reproduced here — the survey itself is the cleaner citation.


## The historical arc

Over the last forty years the direction of travel is unambiguous.
Auckland dwelling prices rose roughly fivefold in real terms
between 1985 and 2022 before a modest correction and shallow
recovery. Real wages roughly doubled over the same period. The
ratio of the two therefore rose from three or four times median
household income in the mid-1980s to eight or ten times at peak.

Rents, on the other hand, rose roughly threefold in nominal terms
from 2000, with about a quarter of the total rise concentrated in
the 2020 to 2023 window. The homeownership rate among Aucklanders
under forty fell from roughly six in ten in 1986 to roughly four
in ten by the mid-2020s. The deposit gap — the number of years of
median saving required to assemble a typical twenty-per-cent
deposit — expanded from two or three years in the 1990s to eight
to twelve at peak.

These are the long-run facts, and they are what a reader in 2076
will want framed. The year-to-year political contest over whether
prices are up two per cent or down three per cent is noise
against this arc.

The cohort homeownership trajectory can be reconstructed from the five-yearly
[Stats NZ Census](https://www.stats.govt.nz/topics/census) tenure-by-age tables
for the Auckland region. The deposit-gap calculation combines Stats NZ
household-saving data with a median-price series (REINZ or RBNZ's House Price
Index). Both require downloads that aren't yet cleanly pipeable, so a rendered
chart would be misleading in its precision.


## Who bears affordability stress

Aggregate ratios mask large variance. Affordability stress is
distributed unevenly across cohorts, tenures, household types,
and parts of the city.

**By generation.** The 30-year decline in young-adult homeownership
is the clearest distributional story. A 30-year-old Aucklander in
1990 could plausibly buy; a 30-year-old in 2025 typically cannot,
without capital assistance. This is a cohort effect that compounds
over decades of accumulated wealth or its absence.

**By tenure.** Renters pay more per square metre than owner-occupiers
on equivalent properties, receive weaker legal protections, and
build no equity. The policy literature on tenure equity is
substantial; the political weight historically given to it is
small. Over the long run the tenure-mix share of renting is rising,
which slowly changes the constituency for tenure-protection policy.

**By household composition.** Single-parent households and older
single renters face the worst affordability stress on
residual-income measures. Aggregate affordability discussion tends
to centre dual-income partnered households, which are atypical of
those most affected.

**By geography within Auckland.** Affordability stress concentrates
in parts of the city where rents are not low enough relative to
the incomes of the households who live there — not always the
same as the parts with the cheapest housing in absolute terms.

Quartile-level rent burden can be computed from [MBIE's rental bond
data](https://catalogue.data.govt.nz/dataset/rental-bond-data-by-region)
against Stats NZ household-income tables. Bond data is an open CSV; the
income-by-quartile split needs a deeper Stats NZ extract that is not yet
automated, so no chart here.


## Drivers specific to affordability

The Land page named geography, title, and regulation as durable
drivers. Supply Economics named cyclicality, productivity, and
financing. The drivers that belong specifically on this page —
because they operate at the outcome level rather than on stock or
flow — are these.

- **Wage–price decoupling.** Over forty years prices and rents
  have grown substantially faster than wages. The ratio problem
  is arithmetically resolvable only by closing that gap.
- **Credit-cycle dominance.** Short-run price movements are driven
  more by interest rates, macroprudential rules, and bank-lending
  practice than by short-run supply. The 2021 low-rate credit
  surge drove prices higher than any plausible supply response
  could have matched.
- **Tax-advantaged housing asset.** The absence of a comprehensive
  capital gains tax, the effective tax-free status of imputed
  rent, and historical deductibility of rental expenses compound
  over decades into the concentrated housing-wealth structure the
  outcome reflects.
- **Intergenerational wealth transfer.** As prices rose past what
  typical earnings can assemble within a working life, family
  assistance and inheritance became structural preconditions for
  first-home buying. Intergenerational wealth is a normal feature
  of property-owning societies, but New Zealand's intergenerational
  wealth base is comparatively young — a couple of centuries for
  European settler families, longer but more fragmented for Māori
  — which shapes how the pattern plays out locally.
- **Tenure legal architecture.** The legal framework governing
  rental tenure — tenancy length, grounds for termination, rent
  rules — shapes what affordability feels like for renters,
  independent of headline rent levels. Renters face stress that
  is partly a cost problem and partly an insecurity problem.


## International comparators

Four archetypes of affordability *regime* are worth comparing —
distinct from the *supply* regimes on the Supply Economics page,
though related. Each reveals a different answer to the ratio
problem.

**Germany and Switzerland — regulated long-term rental.** A
majority-rental society, with strong tenant protections and
rent predictability stretching across decades. Affordability is
primarily managed through the rental regime, not through
ownership subsidy. Stable for a century; not easily transplanted
to a single-family-ownership culture.

**Vienna and Nordic social housing.** Large-scale public and
cooperative housing with income-related rents absorbs the lower
half of the income distribution out of the market. Affordability
is managed through the **market share** of non-market housing.

**Singapore HDB.** Centralised delivery: the state builds, the
state allocates, homeownership is widespread but on regulated
terms. A singular political economy that shows what centralised
affordability delivery can achieve, without easily transplanting.

**Anglo-American ownership society.** Australia, United States,
United Kingdom, Canada, New Zealand. Affordability managed (often
poorly) through first-home subsidies, low-interest-rate regimes,
and expectations of capital-gain-driven wealth accumulation. The
regime Auckland sits inside.

**France.** A hybrid with substantial social housing
(around seventeen per cent), inclusionary-zoning rules (the SRU
law), and active government intervention in the private market.
A useful middle-ground reference.

The engineering question is which archetype Auckland's long-run
trajectory is drifting toward. The current answer is probably
"still Anglo, with gradual drift toward the French hybrid as
non-market builders grow". The drift is not locked in.

The [Demographia International Housing Affordability
Survey](http://www.demographia.com/db-dhi-index.pdf) publishes a directly
comparable PTI ratio for the same set of cities each January, and the
[OECD Affordable Housing Database](https://www.oecd.org/housing/data/affordable-housing-database/)
extends the cross-country coverage. Auckland consistently sits near the
top of the severely-unaffordable range alongside Sydney and London.


## Durable features

Features of the Auckland affordability system that a reader in 2076
or 2126 will still recognise, presented without ranking.

1. **Ratio-based constraint.** Affordability is always a ratio
   between cost and earnings, not either in isolation.
2. **Distributional, not aggregate.** Aggregate affordability
   hides large variance between cohorts, tenures, household types,
   and geographies.
3. **Tenure law shapes outcomes.** The legal architecture of
   renting materially affects what affordability feels like,
   independent of headline rents.
4. **Homeownership rate is a political commons.** A falling
   ownership rate changes the political constituency for price
   stability over decades.
5. **Intergenerational wealth is a structural precondition for
   ownership.** The dynamic will continue while the ratio remains
   high.
6. **Demand-side transfers risk landlord capture.** In
   supply-inelastic markets, income transfers flow partially into
   rents.
7. **Rent regulation is design-dependent.** International
   evidence shows both short-run tenant protection and possible
   long-run supply effects; the balance depends on the specifics
   of the regime.
8. **Credit cycles dominate short-run prices.** Monetary and
   macroprudential policy shape affordability outcomes at higher
   frequency than any housing-specific lever.
9. **Tax advantages compound.** Untaxed capital gains on
   owner-occupied housing compound into generational wealth and
   political resistance to change.
10. **Climate exposure will reshape the affordability map.** Over
    fifty years, affordable housing in flood-prone or
    sea-level-exposed areas becomes uninsurable and eventually
    unlivable, shifting which parts of the city are both cheap
    and viable.


## The durable long-run questions

Questions that matter on a 50- or 100-year frame regardless of who
is in government:

- What target affordability ratio (PTI, rent-to-income, residual
  income) should Auckland aim for, and is it realistic given the
  city's physical and market constraints?
- How much of the affordability outcome should be delivered by
  the market versus by non-market housing at scale?
- What rental-tenure architecture produces durable affordability
  for renters without suppressing supply?
- How should New Zealand manage the structural role of
  intergenerational wealth in household formation?
- Should affordability be primarily income-side (wages,
  transfers) or housing-side (supply, regulation, subsidy)?
- What is the right balance between owner-occupier and renter
  protections over the long run?
- How should the climate-driven shift in where housing is viable
  be managed without concentrating affordability stress in unsafe
  areas?
- Can Auckland afford its own affordability policy without
  national-level tax and transfer reform?

The rest of the page presents the camps that attempt to answer
these. They overlap; serious policy programmes combine elements
of several.

## Solution camps

A number of distinct positions recur in Auckland's land debate. Each is defensible on its own terms, none is obviously correct, and in practice most real policy programmes combine elements of several. They are presented here in alphabetical order and without ranking.


### Income-side — transfers and wages

Affordability is a ratio between housing cost and income; the cleanest long-run lever is the income side. Direct transfers (an Accommodation Supplement uprated to current rents, income-related rents in state and community housing) address the lower end; structural wage growth through productivity and labour-market reform addresses the middle and upper end.

**Flagship moves:**

- Uprate the Accommodation Supplement to track current market rents.
- Expand income-related rents across state and community housing.
- Raise the statutory minimum wage and pursue living-wage policy.
- Invest in productivity growth to raise real wages over time.
- Reform the tax-transfer system to reduce the effective marginal tax rate on low-to-middle incomes.

**Tensions:**

- In supply-inelastic markets, cash transfers can be partially captured by landlords as higher rents.
- Wage growth is an outcome of the economy, not a policy dial.
- Productivity investment has decade-long payback.
- Fiscal cost of expanded transfers.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Direct income transfers targeted at housing cost at the lower end of the income distribution. (state variable: `rent_to_income_ratio`, sign: -)
- Structural wage growth through productivity, training, and labour-market reform. (state variable: `price_to_income_ratio`, sign: -)

**Advocates:** The Helen Clark Foundation; Unions and the living-wage movement; Parts of the welfare-economics academic literature; NZ Productivity Commission (on the productivity side).


### Ownership reform and tax

The structural barrier to ownership and the compounding of housing wealth require structural responses: first-home pathways (shared equity, rent-to-own, KiwiSaver access), inclusionary zoning that delivers below-market product through planning rules, and comprehensive tax reform (land value tax, capital gains tax, inheritance framework) that changes the compounding dynamic over decades.

**Flagship moves:**

- Shared-equity schemes and state-backed low-deposit first-home pathways.
- Expanded KiwiSaver access for first-home deposits.
- Inclusionary zoning rules requiring affordable shares in large developments (the French SRU archetype).
- A comprehensive capital gains tax including on owner-occupied dwellings.
- A land value tax on site value.
- Review of intergenerational wealth transfer, including the absence of inheritance tax.

**Tensions:**

- First-home subsidies often capitalise into prices, benefiting sellers more than buyers.
- Inclusionary zoning raises the cost of market housing at the margin and delivers only a small share of total supply.
- Comprehensive tax reform on housing is politically difficult in every comparable country.
- Transition effects on current owners, including retirees dependent on housing wealth.
- Substantial overlap with the Tax Reform subpage; detail lives there.

**Interventions on the system:**

- First-home pathways and alternative tenure forms to bridge the gap between renting and full ownership. (state variable: `homeownership_rate_by_cohort`, sign: +)
- Structural tax reform on housing as an asset (LVT, CGT, inheritance). (state variable: `price_to_income_ratio`, sign: -) (relaxes: `tax_advantaged_housing_asset`)

**Advocates:** The Opportunities Party (on LVT); Max Rashbrooke and inequality-focused commentators; Parts of the academic tax community; International reference cases — France (inclusionary zoning), Singapore (shared equity).


### Rental tenure architecture

Affordability for the roughly one-in-three Auckland households who rent is as much about security and predictability as about the headline rent level. A European-style rental regime — longer tenure, constrained termination, rent predictability over decades, and growth of institutional build-to-rent landlords — would change what affordability means for renters, independent of market rent movements.

**Flagship moves:**

- Statutory long-tenancy frameworks with grounds-based termination only.
- Rent-increase rules tied to a predictable index rather than market movements.
- Tax and regulatory settings conducive to large-scale institutional build-to-rent.
- Tenant registries and dispute-resolution reform.
- Transition pathways for individual landlords who no longer wish to operate under the regime.

**Tensions:**

- International evidence on rent regulation is mixed — tenant protection in the short run, potential supply suppression in the long run.
- Private landlord exit at the margin if regulation is too restrictive.
- Cultural resistance in a homeowner-oriented society; political opposition from existing landlords.
- Design-dependent — the same name (rent regulation) covers regimes that work very differently.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Legal architecture for long-term, predictable rental tenure. (state variable: `residual_income_after_housing`, sign: +) (relaxes: `political_acceptability_of_rent_regulation`)
- Shift the rental market toward large institutional landlords through BTR tax and regulatory settings. (state variable: `tenure_mix`, sign: 0)

**Advocates:** Renters United and tenant advocacy groups; International reference cases — Germany, Austria, Switzerland, Netherlands; Build-to-rent industry associations (on the BTR dimension specifically).
