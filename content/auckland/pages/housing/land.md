# Land


## Introduction

Housing in Auckland begins with the ground itself. Before any argument about zoning,
affordability, or the role of the state, there is a specific stretch of land — an isthmus
between two harbours, punctuated by volcanic cones, bounded by ranges to the west and south,
and surrounded by one of the longest coastlines of any urban region in the world. That
ground has a legal and cultural history that precedes the modern city by centuries.
Everything that follows in this section — supply economics, affordability, homelessness,
public housing, Māori housing, tax reform, infrastructure, climate, and the quality of the
existing stock — rests on the substrate described here.

The discussion that follows is deliberately structural. It emphasises features of Auckland's
land that will still be recognisable to a reader fifty or one hundred years from now.
Current political debates are mentioned only where they illustrate a durable tension, not as
ends in themselves.


## The physical substrate

Auckland sits on a narrow isthmus, roughly two kilometres wide at its narrowest point,
between the Waitematā harbour to the east and the Manukau harbour to the west. That
geometry is permanent on any civilisational timescale, and it is the single most
consequential fact about the city's land. It concentrates transport, amenity, and
commercial gravity onto a thin strip, forces every north–south journey through a small
number of chokepoints, and makes any land close to either harbour structurally scarce.

The isthmus is also the surface expression of the **Auckland Volcanic Field**, a collection
of roughly fifty scoria cones, maars, and tuff rings erupted over the past quarter of a
million years. The field is geologically active rather than extinct; future eruptions are
considered a low-probability but real component of the region's long-term hazard profile.
Many of the cones are protected as reserves and viewshafts, and the 2014 Tāmaki Makaurau
Collective settlement places fourteen of the most significant maunga under the
co-governance of the Tūpuna Maunga Authority — a legal arrangement designed to endure.

To the west, the **Waitākere Ranges** (approximately sixteen thousand hectares) and, to the
south-east, the **Hunua Ranges** (approximately seventeen thousand hectares) are regional
parks. They are, for practical purposes, permanently removed from the developable land
base. The same is broadly true of the Hauraki Gulf islands gazetted as reserves and of the
extensive marine and coastal park systems.

The Auckland region has a coastline of several thousand kilometres, exceptionally long for
a metropolitan area of its size. A significant fraction of that coastline sits at
elevations that are vulnerable on fifty- and hundred-year horizons. Projections from the
National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research suggest sea-level rise in the order of
0.3 to 1.2 metres by 2100, depending on the global emissions pathway. The practical
consequence is that parts of the low-lying Tāmaki estuary, Māngere, the western reaches of
the Manukau harbour, and a number of north-shore bays are already on trajectories toward
higher flood frequency, reduced insurability, and eventual managed retreat. The January
2023 Auckland Anniversary floods made the existing flood-plain maps politically visible in
a way they had not previously been.

The topography of the region is less flat than its administrative footprint suggests.
Ridgelines, steep valleys, unstable soils in the west, and extensive alluvial flood plains
across the south and north-west all reduce the share of land on which housing can be built
at low infrastructure cost. For planning purposes, the region's *physically developable*
land base is smaller than its total area — and is shrinking at the coastal margin even as
the population grows.


## Tangata whenua and the long historical arc

Tāmaki Makaurau — "Tāmaki desired by many" — has been one of the most contested and most
populous parts of Aotearoa for centuries. Its twin-harbour portage, volcanic soils, fishing
grounds, and strategic position made it valuable to a number of iwi and hapū whose
interests overlap rather than divide cleanly.

Iwi with mana whenua interests in the Auckland isthmus and its surrounds include Ngāti
Whātua (with Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei in the central isthmus), Te Kawerau ā Maki in the west,
Ngāi Tai ki Tāmaki in the east and inner Gulf, Ngāti Pāoa and the wider Marutūāhu
confederation in the east and Hauraki, Waikato–Tainui in the south and south-west, and
Te Ākitai Waiohua in the Māngere and Ihumātao areas, among others with specific site-based
interests. Overlapping interests are the norm rather than the exception, and any framing
that implies a single mana whenua voice for the region is incorrect.

The arc from pre-colonial land use, through nineteenth-century Crown purchases, the 1860s
wars, the Native Land Court's individualisation of customary title, twentieth-century
urban displacement, and the Treaty-settlement process since the 1990s is too large to do
justice to on a housing page. Three points matter for the purposes of this section.

First, by the late twentieth century the overwhelming majority of Auckland region land had
passed out of Māori hands, with the single largest mechanism of loss being the
individualisation and piecemeal sale of customary title through the Native Land Court from
1865 onward, not direct confiscation.

Second, Treaty settlements from the 1990s to the present have returned meaningful — though
modest relative to historical loss — land, commercial redress, and cultural instruments.
Post-settlement iwi are now significant Auckland landowners and developers. Ngāti Whātua
Ōrākei has built and operates several hundred homes at Ōrākei, with further development
planned; multiple iwi hold commercial property portfolios and are increasingly active in
master-planned housing.

Third, this is a durable structural shift. Over fifty- and hundred-year horizons, iwi are
likely to be among the most consequential long-term landowners and developers in the
region, because unlike most private landowners they are intergenerationally constrained —
land returned through settlement is held for descendants, not for resale and exit.

A fuller treatment of mana whenua perspectives on land and housing is the subject of a
dedicated sub-page and is deliberately reserved here.


## Contemporary patterns of ownership

Auckland's land is held by a heterogeneous mix of actors whose time horizons and
motivations differ substantially. In rough terms:

- **Private freehold** dominates the residential footprint and much of the rural
  surrounds. It is highly fragmented — hundreds of thousands of small titles — which makes
  the assembly of development-sized parcels expensive and slow.
- **Auckland Council and its council-controlled organisations** hold the regional and
  local park estate, community facilities, stormwater reserves, water and wastewater
  infrastructure land through Watercare, the port land, and the transport corridors.
  Measured by strategic significance, the Council is arguably the single most consequential
  landowner in the region.
- **The Crown** holds the Department of Conservation estate (smaller in Auckland than in
  the South Island), the Kāinga Ora public-housing portfolio, state highway land,
  Defence land, and the school estate.
- **Iwi and post-settlement entities** hold an expanding portfolio concentrated in specific
  areas including Ōrākei, the Ihumātao vicinity, parts of south Auckland, and the Hauraki
  Gulf islands.
- **Māori freehold land** under Te Ture Whenua Māori Act is a small share of the region,
  typically multiply-owned, with development heavily constrained by title complexity rather
  than by physical capacity.
- **Heritage trusts, churches, universities, and hospitals** hold non-trivial portfolios
  that are often protected from sale.
- **Landbanked private holdings** — zoned-but-undeveloped land held for capital
  appreciation rather than use — matter particularly at the metropolitan edge, in the
  Future Urban Zone.

Two structural features of this pattern stand out. The first is **fragmentation**: the
sheer number of small private titles means that any proposal which requires assembling
contiguous parcels — for large-scale intensification, infrastructure corridors, or
master-planned development — runs into high transaction costs that are not easily reduced
by regulation alone. The second is **title-locked land**: significant areas cannot easily
change use on any reasonable timescale. Treaty-settlement lands with cultural covenants,
heritage-listed sites, regional parks, the tūpuna maunga, and DOC reserves should be
understood as permanent fixtures of the developable map, not contingent ones.


## The regulatory layer

Zoning and land-use regulation sit on top of physical land and decide what can be built
where. The full history of Auckland's planning regimes is a story for another page; the
points that matter for a structural view are these.

Until 2016, land-use rules in the Auckland region were the patchwork of eight separate
district plans inherited from the pre-amalgamation councils. The 2016 **Auckland Unitary
Plan** consolidated these into a single instrument, introduced a hierarchy of residential
zones designed to enable intensification, and upzoned a large portion of the existing
urban footprint. On current evidence, it is the most consequential land-use decision made
in the region in a generation.

Layered over the Unitary Plan are a number of overlays and boundaries that matter
structurally:

- **Special character overlays** restrict demolition and form in heritage areas of the
  inner isthmus. Once designated, such overlays have historically been very difficult to
  remove; a reader in 2076 should expect many of today's character areas still to exist.
- **Viewshafts** radiate from a number of volcanic cones and constrain building height in
  specific corridors.
- A **Rural Urban Boundary** separates the area where urban development is expected from
  the land where it is not. Its position is politically contested and periodically
  revised.
- The **Future Urban Zone** designates land earmarked for eventual urbanisation, typically
  awaiting infrastructure investment and structure planning.

Underneath all of this sits the **Resource Management Act** (1991), currently being
replaced. On any century-scale view, the near-certainty is that whatever statute replaces
the RMA will itself be replaced again within fifty years. Regulatory regimes come and go;
the physical inventory of categories — character, maunga, harbour-adjacent, flood-prone,
Māori-title-constrained, iwi-settlement — persists far longer than the particular statutes
that govern them.


## Features that persist across political cycles

A reader in 2076 or 2126 will still recognise most of the following:

1. **The isthmus geometry and the twin harbours.** Permanent on any meaningful timescale.
   Everything routes around them.
2. **The volcanic field and its viewshafts.** The cones are culturally and legally
   protected; the viewshafts will persist.
3. **The Waitākere and Hunua ranges as land banks of last resort.** Conservation estate is
   politically sticky.
4. **Climate-driven loss of coastal developable land.** The trajectory accelerates through
   the century.
5. **Heritage character zones in the inner isthmus.** Once designated, near-impossible to
   remove at scale.
6. **Fragmented private freehold.** High assembly costs will continue to shape what gets
   built, and where.
7. **Iwi as permanent, intergenerational landowners.** A growing share, a long time
   horizon, and — uniquely among major landowners — no exit option.
8. **Auckland Council as the largest strategic landowner.** Through parks, reserves,
   corridors, and its CCOs, the portfolio outlasts any particular elected administration.
9. **Central Crown holdings** in housing, transport, defence, and education. Subject to
   national political cycles, but slow to change in aggregate.
10. **Regulatory regime instability.** The specific statute changes; the underlying
    question of how to allocate land-use rights does not.


## The durable questions this section cannot resolve

The following are the long-run questions that still matter in 2076 regardless of who is in
government. A defensible public document on Auckland's land can describe them, set out the
evidence, and present the camps. It cannot, and should not, resolve them.

- How much of Auckland's land base should be permanently held in conservation, open space,
  or ecological function, and what is being traded off against housing supply, carbon,
  biodiversity, amenity, and climate resilience?
- What is the right long-run split between intensification of the existing urban footprint
  and greenfield expansion at the metropolitan edge or in concentrated satellite towns?
- How can the development of multiply-owned Māori land be enabled without alienating it,
  and who decides?
- Who captures the uplift when land changes use — the landowner, the developer, the
  community, or the Crown through land-value taxation or a betterment levy?
- How is climate-driven managed retreat to be funded, governed, and allocated over the
  coming century? Who pays, and who stays?
- Should urban-limit regulation be tighter, to protect ecology and force intensification,
  or looser, to deliver cheaper housing at the margin?
- What is the right long-run ownership mix between private freehold, iwi, community, and
  public hands?
- How are the values of place and permanence — cones, heritage, mana whenua sites,
  long-standing neighbourhoods — to be weighed against the urgency of the housing need?

These questions are the spine of the Housing section. Every subsequent sub-page can be
read as an attempt to inform a particular slice of them.

## Solution camps

A number of distinct positions recur in Auckland's land debate. Each is defensible on its own terms, none is obviously correct, and in practice most real policy programmes combine elements of several. They are presented here in alphabetical order and without ranking.


### Compact city / intensification-first

Auckland should accommodate growth within its existing urban footprint, because peripheral land is ecologically and economically costly to urbanise.

**Flagship moves:**

- Hold or tighten the Rural Urban Boundary.
- Remove remaining special character overlays.
- Upzone aggressively along rapid-transit corridors.
- Strengthen density bonuses near transit nodes.

**Tensions:**

- Heritage preservation and character area politics.
- Rural and lifestyle-block landowner expectations.
- Household preferences for detached housing.
- Construction-sector capacity to deliver dense product at scale.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Increase zoned capacity within existing urban footprint. (state variable: `zoned_capacity_dwellings`, sign: +)
- Remove character overlays to allow demolition and redevelopment of heritage stock. (state variable: `developable_area_hectares`, sign: +) (relaxes: `heritage_and_character_overlays`)

**Advocates:** Greater Auckland commentariat; Ryan Greenaway-McGrevy and collaborators; Parts of The Helen Clark Foundation; Parts of Treasury.


### Conservation-first / biophysical limits

A growing share of Auckland's land should be permanently returned to, or retained in, ecological function, and urban density should absorb the resulting growth.

**Flagship moves:**

- Expand regional parks and conservation reserves.
- Make the Rural Urban Boundary effectively permanent.
- Offer private-land conservation incentives such as QEII-style covenants.
- Introduce biodiversity-net-gain requirements for new development.

**Tensions:**

- Housing supply and affordability in the short to medium term.
- Equity implications of foreclosing peripheral expansion for first-home buyers.
- Opportunity cost of agricultural and horticultural land.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Permanently lock the Rural Urban Boundary against outward expansion. (state variable: `developable_area_hectares`, sign: -)
- Expand biodiversity protections onto private land via covenants and incentives. (state variable: `ownership_distribution_by_actor`, sign: 0)

**Advocates:** Forest & Bird; QEII National Trust; Sections of the academic ecology community; Some iwi environmental arms.


### Devolved governance / institutional reform

The binding long-run constraint is the Crown–Council fiscal relationship and Auckland's limited ability to finance its own strategy on land and infrastructure.

**Flagship moves:**

- Crown-backed wholesale borrowing for Council infrastructure investment.
- Expanded land-value-capture tools for rezoning uplift.
- Greater constitutional autonomy for Auckland within the national system.
- Regional-level fiscal instruments (infrastructure levies, targeted rates).

**Tensions:**

- National-government political economy and central-local dynamics.
- Inter-regional equity across the rest of New Zealand.
- Accountability risks of concentrating power at metropolitan level.
- Credit-rating and sovereign-debt implications.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Enable Auckland Council to borrow at Crown-equivalent wholesale rates for infrastructure. (state variable: `zoned_capacity_dwellings`, sign: +) (relaxes: `resource_management_statutory_regime`)
- Introduce a land-value-capture levy on rezoning uplift. (state variable: `ownership_distribution_by_actor`, sign: 0)

**Advocates:** Sections of local-government reform advocates; Some infrastructure economists; Elements of the Auckland Council executive over time.


### Greenfield expansion

Intensification alone cannot deliver enough housing at acceptable cost, and the metropolitan boundary must move outward to absorb demand.

**Flagship moves:**

- Release Future Urban Zone land faster.
- Extend the Rural Urban Boundary.
- Invest in infrastructure to greenfield areas.
- Underwrite edge development via targeted rates and financing.

**Tensions:**

- Ecology and biodiversity loss on the urban fringe.
- Carbon emissions from car-dependent peripheral development.
- Infrastructure cost per dwelling at the margin.
- Loss of productive rural and horticultural land.
- Climate-exposed nature of many candidate expansion areas.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Accelerate release of Future Urban Zone for urbanisation. (state variable: `developable_area_hectares`, sign: +) (relaxes: `resource_management_statutory_regime`)
- Extend the Rural Urban Boundary outward. (state variable: `developable_area_hectares`, sign: +) (relaxes: `resource_management_statutory_regime`)

**Advocates:** Development industry associations; Some outer-suburb local boards; Parts of the affordability advocacy community.


### Land-market reform / value taxation

The structure of land rights and taxation, not zoning alone, determines how land is used over the long run.

**Flagship moves:**

- Land value tax applied annually on site value.
- Betterment levy on rezoning uplift.
- Vacant-land tax on undeveloped zoned residential land.
- Reform of heritage compensation frameworks.

**Tensions:**

- Resistance from existing landowners, including asset-rich retirees.
- Practical challenges of mass valuation in the New Zealand context.
- Political feasibility of shifting the tax base toward land.
- Transition effects on current owners under pre-existing expectations.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Introduce a land value tax to incentivise productive use of zoned land. (state variable: `title_fragmentation_index`, sign: -)
- Levy rezoning uplift to fund associated infrastructure. (state variable: `ownership_distribution_by_actor`, sign: 0)

**Advocates:** The Opportunities Party (TOP); Parts of the NZ Initiative on LVT specifically; Max Rashbrooke and related inequality-focused commentators; Elements of the academic tax community.


### Māori-led land use / papakāinga

Significant housing capacity exists on iwi-owned and Māori freehold land, and the barriers are primarily regulatory and financial rather than physical.

**Flagship moves:**

- Reform Te Ture Whenua Māori Act to enable development without alienation.
- Crown funding and finance for papakāinga and iwi-led master plans.
- Crown role as co-investor rather than deliverer.
- Streamlined consenting for Māori-land developments.

**Tensions:**

- Expectations of non-Māori landowners about parity of regulatory treatment.
- Pace of Treaty-settlement implementation.
- Capacity constraints of iwi development arms.
- Durability of legal protections against future alienation.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Reform Te Ture Whenua to unlock multiply-owned Māori land for development. (state variable: `zoned_capacity_dwellings`, sign: +) (relaxes: `te_ture_whenua_multiply_owned_maori_land`)
- Crown-funded papakāinga infrastructure and finance. (state variable: `ownership_distribution_by_actor`, sign: 0)

**Advocates:** Te Matapihi; Iwi housing arms and CHPs; Te Puni Kōkiri programmes; Te Pāti Māori.


### Polycentric / satellite growth

Neither centralised intensification nor unbounded edge sprawl is optimal; growth should be concentrated in defined nodes such as Warkworth, Kumeu, Pukekohe, and Drury.

**Flagship moves:**

- Structure plans directed to named growth centres.
- Rapid-transit investment linking nodes to the core.
- Hold rural land between nodes in semi-rural use.
- Cluster employment, services, and density at node cores.

**Tensions:**

- High infrastructure cost of building up multiple centres in parallel.
- Risk of low-density sprawl filling gaps between nodes if boundaries are not held.
- Transit capital cost and timing.
- Employment follows density imperfectly on decadal timescales.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Concentrate growth at defined nodes with rapid-transit connectivity. (state variable: `zoned_capacity_dwellings`, sign: +)
- Hold agricultural and ecological land between nodes through zoning. (state variable: `developable_area_hectares`, sign: 0) (relaxes: `resource_management_statutory_regime`)

**Advocates:** Some regional planners; Auckland Council Future Development Strategy authors over time.
