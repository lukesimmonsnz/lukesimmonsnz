# Supply Economics


## Introduction

Land is the stock. Supply Economics is the flow. The Land sub-page
described what ground is available in Auckland, who owns it, and how
it is zoned. This page picks up one level downstream: the system that
produces new dwellings on top of that ground — who builds, at what
cost, under what constraints, and in response to what signals.

On a 10-, 50-, or 100-year horizon, the headline-political question
of whether supply was high or low in any particular year is
uninteresting. What matters is the **shape** of the system — its
actors, its cost structure, its feedback loops, and the binding
constraints that will still apply when today's political cycle has
long passed.


## Scale and history

Auckland consents roughly ten to twenty thousand new dwellings per
year, and completions lag consents by a year or two and fall short of
them by a modest margin. The region's stock is in the order of six
hundred thousand dwellings, and net additions — the difference
between new builds and demolitions — are small relative to that
base. Even at sustained high build rates, the built environment
turns over slowly.

The most visible structural shift over the last decade has been in
**typology**. Historically, Auckland housing was detached-dominated:
the suburban detached house on its own section was the default
dwelling. Since the 2016 Auckland Unitary Plan, consents have
shifted sharply toward multi-unit housing — townhouses and
apartments now make up the majority of new consents. This is the
single clearest supply-side signal that upzoning works, whatever
debates remain about magnitude.

Auckland's supply has also always been **cyclical**. The sector
boom-busts on roughly decadal rhythms, with downturns after the
1970s oil shock, the 1987 market crash, the 2008 Global Financial
Crisis, and a further contraction from 2022. Each downturn destroys
productive capacity — firms exit, skilled workers leave the
industry, apprenticeships are abandoned — and that capacity is slow
to rebuild when the next upturn arrives.

Stats NZ publishes the full long-run series as the monthly [Building consents
issued](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/?filters=Building+consents+issued)
release, with a regional split that isolates Auckland. Auckland Council's
research unit (RIMU) tracks the typology split — detached, townhouse,
apartment — through its [Knowledge Auckland](https://www.knowledgeauckland.org.nz/)
portal. Those feeds tell the same cyclical-with-a-post-2016-shift story
the text above describes; a chart here would add volume without adding
substance until the data can be pulled cleanly.

The price-versus-consents lag is visible month by month in REINZ's [monthly
property reports](https://www.reinz.co.nz/news-and-insight) plotted against the
Stats NZ [building consents release](https://www.stats.govt.nz/information-releases/) — a long-run chart here would need licensed REINZ
or CoreLogic data, so the series is not reproduced on this page.


## Who builds

Auckland's supply is delivered by a heterogeneous mix of actors with
very different time horizons, financial structures, and motivations.
This heterogeneity is a durable structural feature of the system.

- **Small-scale residential builders** — one to twenty houses a year,
  historically the majority of detached supply, highly cyclical, and
  first to exit on every downturn.
- **Mid-size developers** specialising in townhouses and small
  apartments. Fastest-growing segment since the Unitary Plan enabled
  intensification zones.
- **Large-scale developers** — apartment towers, master-planned
  communities. Capital-intensive, concentrated in a handful of
  firms, dependent on pre-sales and project finance.
- **Group-home builders** such as Fletcher Living, GJ Gardner, and
  Mike Greer. National scale, standardised product, modest
  productivity gains from volume.
- **Kāinga Ora**, the Crown public-housing builder. A significant
  delivery channel through the 2020s, and politically cyclical.
- **Community Housing Providers** — third-sector builders,
  including iwi-linked entities, growing as a share of the total.
- **Iwi development arms** — Ngāti Whātua Ōrākei, Tainui Group
  Holdings, and regional iwi entities. Intergenerationally
  motivated; they cannot sell and exit the way a private
  speculator can.
- **Owner-builders and self-builders**. A small but genuine
  component of detached supply, long under-counted.

Alongside these sits a category that is **under-described in the
policy literature**: intergenerational family trusts. Under New
Zealand's Trusts Act, beneficiary information is protected, which
means a meaningful share of significant land and property assets
is held in trust vehicles that do not appear in public statistics.
Structurally these trusts behave more like iwi than like private
speculators — long-horizon, typically non-exiting, often with
explicit intergenerational or conservation mandates written into
their deeds. Over 50- and 100-year windows they are a real
delivery and custodianship vector, even though public-facing
analyses rarely name them.

The durable point is that no single actor dominates. This is both
a source of resilience — no single-point failure can collapse
delivery — and a coordination problem, since no single policy
lever can move the aggregate supply rate at will.


## The cost stack

A new Auckland dwelling's delivered cost is a stack of distinct
components, each with its own drivers and its own long-run
behaviour. Structural categories matter more than point figures,
because the point figures shift with every interest-rate cycle and
every commodity shock; the structure of the stack is the durable
object.

| Component                                        | Driver of variation                                    |
|--------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|
| Serviced land                                    | Location, zoning, infrastructure, title fragmentation  |
| Hard construction cost                           | Materials, labour, complexity, code requirements       |
| Finance cost during build                        | Interest rates, project duration, debt/equity mix      |
| Infrastructure contributions and levies          | Council settings, infrastructure need                  |
| Consenting and design                            | Regulatory complexity, reviewer capacity, appeals      |
| Developer margin required to fund the next project | Risk premium, cost of capital                        |
| GST and other compliance                         | National tax settings                                  |

On a 50-year view, the share of the stack taken by each component
drifts more than its absolute level. Materials and labour have
trended up as a share, because New Zealand construction-sector
labour productivity growth has been close to flat for decades
while the rest of the economy has grown faster. Developer margin
is load-bearing: a project that does not clear roughly fifteen to
twenty per cent margin on cost typically cannot finance the next
one. When costs rise faster than sale prices, margin compresses,
projects get cancelled, and the pipeline thins regardless of the
underlying demand — which is the main mechanism by which housing
affordability crises become self-perpetuating.

## International comparators

The engineering move in a problem like this is to compare. Auckland
is one of a small number of major English-speaking metropolitan
housing markets with broadly similar legal inheritance, and a
larger set of rich-country metropolitan markets that solve the
supply problem differently. Each archetype reveals which of the
constraints described above is most binding in practice.

**Tokyo.** National-level permissive zoning, a large population of
small private builders, and a culture of continual stock
replacement produce the highest supply elasticity of any major
rich-country city. Tokyo's population has been broadly steady for
decades, yet its housing stock is continually renewed. The
archetype shows what happens when regulatory and land constraints
are genuinely relaxed.

**Houston and Dallas.** Permissive land-use, greenfield expansion
at the metropolitan edge, and lightly regulated construction
produce a supply-elastic regime — at the price of car dependence,
infrastructure sprawl, and climate exposure. The archetype shows
the cost of pursuing elasticity primarily through land release.

**Vienna.** A dominant public and cooperative housing sector
delivers around half of new supply. Prices and rents are
stabilised across market cycles by the sheer scale of non-market
delivery. Vienna is the clearest illustration of a supply regime
where **market share**, not market reform, is the lever.

**Singapore.** The HDB, a government builder, has delivered the
majority of Singapore's housing; private freehold is the
exception rather than the default. The model is not easily
transplantable — it rests on specific political and constitutional
foundations — but it is instructive about what non-market delivery
at real scale can achieve.

**London, Sydney, and Melbourne.** Close structural analogues to
Auckland — supply-inelastic, high prices, similar regulatory
politics, similar demographic pressures. Worth comparing directly,
because they are the cities Auckland's problems most resemble and
whose policy experiments Auckland can learn from fastest.

**Nordic prefabrication** — Sweden, Finland, Norway. Heavy use of
factory-built modular components has raised construction
productivity materially. The archetype shows what is possible on
the industrial-transformation front, and also what scale is
required to make prefabrication work.

Auckland's current supply elasticity sits somewhere between its
pre-2016 self and the middle of the rich-country distribution. It
is lower than Tokyo or Houston; it is higher than Sydney or
London. The question for a 50- or 100-year frame is which
archetype Auckland is drifting toward — or whether it will
sustain a distinct hybrid.

City-level supply-elasticity estimates come out of the economics literature
rather than a single downloadable series — the foundational numbers are in
[Saiz (2010)](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/125/3/1253/1903475) and
[Glaeser & Gyourko's collected work](https://www.nber.org/people/edward_glaeser),
with comparable cross-country estimates in the [OECD housing working
papers](https://www.oecd.org/en/topics/sub-issues/housing.html). The pattern
described above survives the methodological differences between those
estimates.


## Durable constraints

Features of the supply system that a reader in 2076 or 2126 will
still recognise. They are presented without ranking; different
observers disagree about which is most binding.

1. **Cyclicality.** The sector boom-busts every seven to fifteen
   years, destroying firms and workforce each time.
2. **Weak productivity growth.** Without structural change,
   construction costs will keep rising relative to the economy.
3. **Labour force constraints.** Construction depends on a
   slow-to-train workforce; skill shortages recur in every boom.
4. **Infrastructure-gated capacity.** Even where zoning permits,
   developments stall on water, wastewater, stormwater, and
   transport capacity.
5. **Finance pro-cyclicality.** Banks tighten lending at the
   moment developers most need finance.
6. **Material import dependence.** New Zealand imports most
   structural materials; global supply shocks feed into local
   costs with limited substitution.
7. **Small domestic market.** Auckland is too small to support
   Tokyo- or Houston-scale standardised mass production;
   productivity gains via scale are structurally limited.
8. **Regulatory regime instability.** Consenting rules, heritage
   overlays, and the broader resource-management regime change on
   decadal timescales.
9. **Political cyclicality of public builders.** Kāinga Ora's
   build rate rises and falls with government, so non-market
   supply is not a full counter-cyclical stabiliser unless its
   funding is insulated from electoral cycles.
10. **Long-horizon builders are the minority.** Iwi development
    arms and intergenerational family trusts are the only actors
    structurally committed to 50-plus-year delivery. Everyone
    else can, and eventually does, exit.


## The durable long-run questions

Questions that still matter in 2076 regardless of who is in
government. A defensible document can describe the terrain and
present the camps; it cannot and should not resolve them.

- What supply elasticity should Auckland aim for, and is that
  achievable given the city's physical and market size?
- How is the boom-bust cycle to be damped — through
  counter-cyclical public build, credit-cycle regulation, or
  accepting it as a fact of life?
- Should the housing-production system rely on a diverse ecosystem
  of builders, or concentrate on a few large actors at scale?
- How is construction productivity to be raised — through
  prefabrication and modern methods of construction, regulatory
  standardisation, skills immigration, or structural
  consolidation?
- What share of new supply should come from non-market actors
  (Crown, community housing providers, iwi, long-horizon trusts)
  on a 50-year horizon?
- How should the building code be balanced between resilience and
  thermal performance on the one hand and affordability on the
  other?
- What financing architecture produces stable delivery across
  cycles?
- How does climate adaptation — insurance pricing, managed retreat
  — re-shape where and what gets built over the century?

The rest of this page presents the solution camps that attempt to
answer these questions. They overlap in practice; a serious policy
programme combines several.

## Solution camps

A number of distinct positions recur in Auckland's land debate. Each is defensible on its own terms, none is obviously correct, and in practice most real policy programmes combine elements of several. They are presented here in alphabetical order and without ranking.


### Finance architecture and cycle management

The bank-debt-and-pre-sales financing model is itself a major source of boom-bust cyclicality, and policy tools that smooth demand (credit-cycle regulation, stable immigration, tax stability) are under-used. New financing architectures — build-to-rent equity, pension capital, infrastructure bonds, a state development bank — would stabilise delivery across cycles.

**Flagship moves:**

- Tax and regulatory settings conducive to long-term build-to-rent equity.
- Channels for KiwiSaver and pension capital into housing delivery.
- A state development bank or equivalent for counter-cyclical construction finance.
- Macroprudential rules explicitly linked to the construction cycle.
- Stable, long-horizon immigration settings that reduce demand shocks.

**Tensions:**

- Untested at New Zealand scale; international precedents are mixed.
- Cross-cutting with national monetary, immigration, and tax policy — requires coordination beyond Auckland.
- May reduce cyclicality at the cost of a lower long-run average build rate.
- Risks distorting what gets built toward products favoured by particular finance vehicles.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Develop finance architectures suited to long-horizon, counter-cyclical housing delivery (BTR equity, pension capital, state development bank). (state variable: `developer_margin`, sign: 0)
- Damp the housing cycle through macroprudential and demand-management tools. (state variable: `annual_consents`, sign: 0)

**Advocates:** Reserve Bank of New Zealand (on macroprudential dimension); Sections of the housing-finance academic literature; Build-to-rent industry associations; International examples (Nordic pension-backed housing; German cooperatives).


### Industrial transformation of construction

The real long-run supply constraint is weak construction-sector productivity. Shifting from site-based craft construction to factory-based prefabrication, standardised components, and a stable workforce pipeline is the only way to materially change the cost of delivering a dwelling in Auckland.

**Flagship moves:**

- Scale prefabrication and off-site manufacturing capacity.
- Standardised dwelling designs suitable for volume production.
- Building code reform to remove barriers to manufactured housing.
- Public procurement as an anchor for domestic prefab demand.
- Expanded trades training, apprenticeships, and skills immigration pathways.

**Tensions:**

- Small domestic market limits the efficiency gains from standardisation.
- Prefabrication has repeatedly over-promised and under-delivered over multiple decades.
- Large up-front capital investment required before productivity gains materialise.
- Workforce training has a decade-long payback and is itself pro-cyclical.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Invest in prefabrication and modern methods of construction to raise sector productivity. (state variable: `construction_cost_index`, sign: -)
- Expand workforce pipeline through training, apprenticeships, and targeted skills immigration. (state variable: `workforce_size`, sign: +) (relaxes: `labour_force_skill_mix`)

**Advocates:** Prefab industry associations; NZ Productivity Commission (historical); OECD construction-productivity analyses; Parts of the infrastructure engineering community.


### Infrastructure-first / trunk capacity

Zoning without infrastructure is theatre. Supply is gated by the capacity of water, wastewater, stormwater, and transport networks. Building trunk capacity ahead of private demand — rather than in response to it — is a precondition for any other supply lever to work.

**Flagship moves:**

- Sustained Crown and Council investment in trunk infrastructure ahead of demand.
- Growth-focused funding for Watercare and equivalent infrastructure entities.
- Land-value-capture and betterment levies to fund up-front infrastructure.
- Long-duration infrastructure bonds and wholesale borrowing arrangements.

**Tensions:**

- Fiscal cost of building capacity before it is used.
- Timing risk — capacity may precede demand by years or decades.
- Inter-regional equity concerns about national funding concentrated on Auckland.
- Political feasibility of pre-investment across electoral cycles.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Fund and build trunk infrastructure ahead of projected demand, not in response to it. (state variable: `annual_consents`, sign: +) (relaxes: `infrastructure_pipeline`)
- Introduce land-value-capture or betterment levies to recycle uplift into infrastructure. (state variable: `developer_margin`, sign: 0)

**Advocates:** Te Waihanga New Zealand Infrastructure Commission; Sections of the local-government reform community; Some infrastructure economists.


### Market reform — regulatory deregulation

The binding constraints on new supply are regulatory consenting time, infrastructure-contribution complexity, and the appeal pathways that delay or kill projects. Remove this friction and the existing market will deliver more housing.

**Flagship moves:**

- Compressed statutory consenting timelines with default-approve thresholds.
- Expanded permitted-activity intensification along rapid-transit corridors.
- Simpler and more predictable development-contribution frameworks.
- Reduction and streamlining of appeal pathways.

**Tensions:**

- Regulatory deregulation cannot overcome hard constraints on labour, land, or infrastructure capacity.
- Risk of lower build quality or weaker environmental standards in the rush to approve.
- Heritage, character, and amenity interests are real and have durable political weight.
- Does not address the finance-side cyclicality that drives project cancellations.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Reduce consenting friction and appeal pathways to shorten time-to-build. (state variable: `annual_consents`, sign: +) (relaxes: `regulatory_consenting_time`)

**Advocates:** Market-oriented economic commentators; Some development industry bodies; Sections of central-government planning reform.


### Non-market delivery at scale

Market supply is pro-cyclical and systematically under-delivers at the bottom of the market. Scaling non-market builders — Kāinga Ora, community housing providers, iwi development arms, and intergenerational private trusts — produces counter-cyclical, durable supply that the market cannot.

**Flagship moves:**

- Recapitalise Kāinga Ora and insulate its mandate from electoral cycles.
- Scale community housing providers with stable long-term finance.
- Crown co-investment in iwi-led housing pipelines.
- Enabling frameworks for intergenerational family trusts and similar long-horizon private vehicles.

**Tensions:**

- Fiscal cost and ongoing political fragility of public builders.
- Historical concerns over Kāinga Ora build cost and efficiency.
- Scale constraints on iwi and CHP sectors over a decade or less.
- Intergenerational trust delivery is largely invisible to policy and hard to design incentives for.

**Interventions on the system:**

- Expand and stabilise non-market builders (state, community, iwi, long-horizon trusts) as a durable delivery channel. (state variable: `share_of_supply_by_actor_type`, sign: +)
- Finance vehicles suited to long-horizon (50-year+) holders rather than short-cycle private capital. (state variable: `developer_margin`, sign: 0)

**Advocates:** Community Housing Aotearoa and the third sector; Te Matapihi and iwi housing advocates; Centre-left housing economists; Sections of international social-housing literature (Vienna archetype).
