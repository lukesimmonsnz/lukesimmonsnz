## Solution camps

A number of distinct positions recur in policy debates on this issue. Each is defensible on its own terms; none is obviously correct. Presented in alphabetical order without ranking.


### Climate-resilient infrastructure and primary sector adaptation

Investing in flood-resilient infrastructure and primary sector adaptation tools reduces climate damage costs over time.

**Flagship moves:**

- Fund cyclone-resilient roading and bridge replacements in vulnerable catchments
- Invest in drought-tolerant crop research and water storage for Northland farming
- Establish community-led climate resilience hubs on marae

**Tensions:**

- Infrastructure investment costs are high relative to Northland's fiscal base
- Adaptation does not address the underlying emissions trajectory
- Agricultural transition timelines exceed many farmers' planning horizons

**Interventions on the system:**

- Fund cyclone-resilient roading and bridge replacements in vulnerable catchments (state variable: `climate_pressure_index`, sign: +)


### Managed retreat and coastal risk planning

Planned relocation of at-risk coastal communities and infrastructure is necessary given the pace of sea-level rise and storm intensification.

**Flagship moves:**

- Develop a Northland coastal managed retreat framework
- Establish a Crown fund for voluntary buyout of high-risk coastal properties
- Restrict new development in coastal inundation zones under district plans

**Tensions:**

- Managed retreat is politically and socially highly contested
- Māori land and wāhi tapu in coastal zones require specific cultural frameworks
- Compensation costs for buyouts are fiscally significant

**Interventions on the system:**

- Develop a Northland coastal managed retreat framework (state variable: `climate_pressure_index`, sign: +)

## Claims cited on this page

- **Northland faces increased drought frequency under climate projections, with dry years shifting from 1-in-6 year to 1-in-4 year recurrence intervals by 2070. Pastoral farming (dominant land use) and rural water supplies face greater reliability pressures, particularly affecting Kaipara district and Far North communities.** *(confidence: medium)* — Northland Regional Council State of the Environment Report 2023.
- **Clay soil properties exacerbate drought impact: rapid hardening, cracking, and reduced water retention when dried. Pasture failure 2–3 times per decade in drought years; stock water supply becomes critical pressure in eastern Northland. Agriculture contribution to regional GDP (dairy 15–20%, beef/sheep 8–12%) makes drought economically significant. Small-to-medium farms lack irrigation infrastructure; dairy intensification increases groundwater demand vulnerability.** *(confidence: medium)* — Stats NZ Northland Regional Profile 2023.
