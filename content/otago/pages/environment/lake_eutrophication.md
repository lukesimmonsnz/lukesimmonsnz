## Solution camps

A number of distinct positions recur in policy debates on this issue. Each is defensible on its own terms; none is obviously correct. Presented in alphabetical order without ranking.


### Lake Water Quality and Algal Bloom Prevention

Integrated lake management (nutrient load reduction, aeration, invasive control) restores ecosystem health.

**Flagship moves:**

- Target 30% nutrient load reduction to Lakes Wanaka and Wakatipu
- Install aeration and oxidation systems where needed
- Control invasive fish species

**Tensions:**

- Nutrient reduction requires upstream agricultural change
- Aeration energy costs and chemical inputs

**Interventions on the system:**

- Install aeration buoys and water quality monitoring in Lakes Wanaka, Wakatipu (state variable: `lake_dissolved_oxygen`, sign: +)


### Pastoral Farming Regulation and Riparian Protection

Mandatory riparian fencing, feed storage regulations, and stock exclusion reduce pastoral nutrient loads.

**Flagship moves:**

- Mandate riparian buffers (minimum 3m) on all waterways
- Regulate intensive grazing near streams
- Subsidize riparian planting

**Tensions:**

- Farmer compliance cost and land-use restrictions
- Enforcement challenges in remote areas

**Interventions on the system:**

- Require riparian fencing and buffer zones by 2030 (state variable: `riparian_coverage`, sign: +)

Edendale Fonterra milk supply depends on Clutha water quality.** *(confidence: medium)* — Otago Regional Council Statistics 2024.
- **Freshwater stress is intensifying: Clutha allocations approaching physical limit (climate variability reducing reliability); Central Otago irrigation heavily dependent on snowmelt and dam releases (Clyde/Roxburgh); drought 2022-2023 stressed schemes; Queenstown Wakatipu lake-level management contested (summer drawdown for irrigation vs recreation); groundwater depletion in schist soils evident; ORC allocation review likely to restrict new consents.** *(confidence: medium)* — Otago Regional Council Statistics 2024.
