## Claims cited on this page

- **Hospital rebuild is on critical path for South Island health system: multi-year project (2024-2030) with escalating costs; operational disruptions during demolition/construction; recruit and retain specialist workforce challenges (competition with other regions); bed capacity during transition period managed via temporary facilities/regional redistribution; post-completion, new facility designed for 2050 population (with growth assumptions for Queenstown, Otago); financing via crown debt / partnership models under negotiation.** *(confidence: medium)* — Otago Regional Council Statistics 2024.
- **Infrastructure deficits accumulating: Dunedin water main breaks increasing (2-3 per month, aging cast iron pipes); Queenstown growth outpacing three waters capacity (development consents now conditional on infrastructure funding); Dunedin Hospital rebuild ($2.3B+) consuming public capital (other assets deferred); road maintenance backlogs on secondary routes (rural Clutha, Waitaki); Forsyth Barr Stadium debt servicing limiting DCC capital for other priorities; earthquake-prone building (EPB) compliance costs rising in Dunedin CBD.** *(confidence: medium)* — Otago Regional Council Statistics 2024.
- **Wakatipu water infrastructure in Queenstown faces compound stress: Lake Wakatipu (Frankton Arm) summer drawdown for irrigation (managed by Meridian/water users); competing demands (irrigation, municipal water supply, recreation, ecological flow); aging treatment plant (capacity 20 ML/day, peak demand approaching); pipeline and storage tank aging; flood risk in Frankton precinct (lake-level management contested); climate risk to inflows (snowmelt variability increasing).** *(confidence: medium)* — Otago Regional Council Statistics 2024.
- **Otago core infrastructure faces aging asset backlogs: Dunedin three waters (water, wastewater, stormwater) network aging (pipes 40-60 years old, replacement rates insufficient); Queenstown three waters under growth pressure (supply, treatment, discharge capacity); ORC bus fleet aging (Dunedin); road network (SH1, SH8 Lindis Pass, SH6 Frankton) maintenance costs rising; Port Chalmers / Port Otago aging container and log-handling facilities; electricity distribution (Meridian, Otago Energy) undergoing network hardening.** *(confidence: medium)* — Otago Regional Council Statistics 2024.
- **Wakatipu water infrastructure under strain: irrigation season drawdown deepening (drought frequency increasing); municipal supply augmentation projects needed (Wilding Tree Landscape investment, new bore field); treatment plant upgrade deferred (cost/funding uncertainty); summer peak demand periods now seeing supply tightness (hosepipe bans 2-3 summers in past decade); flood defences for Frankton Arm being re-evaluated (climate scenarios); water quality (algae in low-flow periods, sediment from hydro operations) declining.** *(confidence: medium)* — Otago Regional Council Statistics 2024.
- **Rural digital infrastructure improving gradually: Crown Fibre Initiative expanding (Wanaka, Central Otago, parts of Waitaki); 5G network expansion into secondary centres (Arrowtown, Cromwell); rural broadband speed and reliability improving but pockets of poor service remain (Catlins, far South Otago). Uptake lagging in older demographic; cost barriers (equipment, plans) for some rural households; agricultural precision farming demand driving enterprise-level fiber demand.** *(confidence: medium)* — Otago Regional Council Statistics 2024.
- **Rural Otago digital infrastructure is mixed: Dunedin/Queenstown have fiber and 5G coverage; rural areas (Cromwell, Arrowtown, South Otago) have fiber rollout underway (Crown Fibre Initiative); remote Catlins (Kaka Point, Owaka) remain broadband-limited (fixed wireless, satellite fallback options). Digital divide in older demographic (low adoption); agriculture and small business increasingly dependent on connectivity; distance learning feasibility varies widely by location.** *(confidence: medium)* — Otago Regional Council Statistics 2024.
- **Dunedin Hospital rebuild is Otago's largest infrastructure project: $2.3B+ (escalated from $1.5B in 2023-2024); new Acute Services Building and Outpatient Services Building; demolition of aging east wing; completion expected 2029-2030. Project employs 200-300 workers (peak construction); disrupts existing hospital operations; regional implications for surgery, ED, mental health bed capacity during transition; economic stimulus (local employment, supplies) significant.** *(confidence: medium)* — Otago Regional Council Statistics 2024.
