## Solution camps

A number of distinct positions recur in policy debates on this issue. Each is defensible on its own terms; none is obviously correct. Presented in alphabetical order without ranking.


### Marine Protected Areas and Ecosystem-Based Management

West Coast's unique coastal and marine ecosystem requires formal protection and integrated management to sustain fisheries, biodiversity, and tourism.

**Flagship moves:**

- Establish West Coast Marine Protected Area network covering key reef and estuarine habitats
- Develop integrated Westland coastal management plan with Poutini Ngāi Tahu
- Enforce commercial fishing limits in sensitive inshore habitats

**Tensions:**

- Fishing industry opposes MPAs that restrict commercial access
- Enforcement capacity on a remote coastline is very limited

**Interventions on the system:**

- Establish West Coast Marine Protected Area network (state variable: `marine_biodiversity_index`, sign: +)


### Mine Site Rehabilitation and Legacy Contamination

Historical coal and gold mining has left contaminated land and water across the West Coast; systematic remediation reduces ongoing ecological harm.

**Flagship moves:**

- Fund systematic audit and rehabilitation of orphaned mine sites
- Require bonded rehabilitation for all new mining consents
- Partner with Poutini Ngāi Tahu to restore taonga species habitat in affected areas

**Tensions:**

- Orphaned mine remediation costs fall on the Crown when operators are dissolved
- Full remediation of all legacy sites would cost hundreds of millions

**Interventions on the system:**

- Fund legacy mine site rehabilitation programme (state variable: `contaminated_site_area_ha`, sign: -)

- **West Coast coast experiences accelerating erosion from sea level rise and increased storm surge frequency. Westport, Greymouth, and Hokitika seafronts face inundation and infrastructure damage risk. Coastal wetlands and estuaries have been degraded by historical mining and logging.
