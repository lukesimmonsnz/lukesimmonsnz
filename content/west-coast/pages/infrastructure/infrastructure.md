## Solution camps

A number of distinct positions recur in policy debates on this issue. Each is defensible on its own terms; none is obviously correct. Presented in alphabetical order without ranking.


### State Highway Resilience and Pass Route Investment

West Coast's three alpine passes are the only road connections to the rest of NZ; NZTA investment in pass resilience and closure alternatives is critical to economic and emergency access.

**Flagship moves:**

- Upgrade Arthur's Pass and Lewis Pass resilience for extreme weather events
- Develop Haast Pass alternative routing feasibility study
- Fund permanent closure response protocols and air access contingency for multi-day closures

**Tensions:**

- Full resilience of all three passes against extreme events is prohibitively expensive
- Low traffic volumes make large capital investment hard to justify purely on transport economics

**Interventions on the system:**

- Fund West Coast alpine pass resilience programme (state variable: `pass_closure_days_per_year`, sign: -)


### Three Waters and Community Infrastructure Renewal

West Coast's small towns have ageing water and wastewater infrastructure that councils cannot afford to renew from local rates; Crown co-investment is essential.

**Flagship moves:**

- Fund three waters renewal in Westport, Hokitika, Reefton, and smaller towns
- Establish West Coast infrastructure revolving fund with Crown backstop
- Develop multi-council joint infrastructure delivery model

**Tensions:**

- Small community infrastructure is expensive per capita and has limited revenue base
- National three waters reform frameworks may not fit West Coast's unique low-density context

**Interventions on the system:**

- Fund West Coast three waters infrastructure renewal programme (state variable: `infrastructure_condition_index`, sign: +)

## Claims cited on this page

- **West Coast infrastructure faces convergent hazards: flooding (5,000-10,000 mm rainfall annually), Alpine Fault seismic risk (M8+ 30% in 50 years), and sea level rise (1.6 mm/year, accelerating). Combined flood and earthquake insurance withdrawal has left many property owners self-insuring. Infrastructure resilience investment is underfunded relative to hazard exposure. Managed retreat and relocation of critical services are necessary but politically contested.** [value: 30 percent Alpine Fault M8+ probability (50-year window); 2020-2070] — West Coast Regional Council Annual Plan 2024; Waka Kotahi State Highway Network – West Coast Region 2023.
